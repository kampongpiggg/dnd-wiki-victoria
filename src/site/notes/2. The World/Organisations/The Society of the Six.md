The **Society of the Six**, more commonly referred to as the Society, or simply the Six, is a private estate on a small hill just outside [[2. The World/Locations/Leonitar\|Leonitar]]. An exclusive club accessible only through a single gated road, it was founded shortly after the formation of the Concord for the young Alastian nobles to mingle and network without the pressures of formal governance; they are expected to learn basic combat skills, form social connections and prepare for their future roles as heirs.

## Description
The Society is a sprawling estate that resembles a grand country manor more than an academic institution. Beautiful buildings cluster around manicured lawns, hidden gardens and shaded courtyards. 

Its centerpiece is the Manor House, which houses the dining hall, lounges, libraries and administrative offices. Characterized by an elegance obtained only by inherited wealth and power, deep leather armchairs and portraits of past members and their great deeds decorate the space.

Beside the Manor House are the Dueling Greens. Perfectly manicured grass bordered by hedges sit below a beautiful pavilion. The young sorcerers are often brought out here to hone their combat capabilities and master their spellcasting. Duels held are not just mere training sessions, they are elaborate social events.

## Dueling Culture
Members often gather on the Green to watch, to wager, and sometimes, to be seen. Formal duels follow incredibly complicated etiquette, but *always* end when first blood is drawn. After all, the duels are not grudge matches, but a display of skills. Wagering on such duels is common place, although members rarely do it for gold. Gold is considered vulgar; members wager favors, introductions and future considerations. A well-placed bet here can yield more than any amount of gold.

## Membership
Scions of the six houses that make up the [[2. The World/Organisations/Chamber of Lords-Advisory\|Chamber of Lords-Advisory]] have full membership, and are expected to attend from ages 12 to 20. Each house is also allotted a certain number of slots per year, negotiated annually in the Chamber. Because of the exclusivity, there is a long waiting list, prompting houses to lobby each other for additional slots, often at the cost of trading favors or forming alliances. 

In extremely rare occasions, an individual who has performed an exceptional service to the Concord may be invited as an honorary member. This has happened once in the Society's history. 

The Society is staffed exclusively from minor branches and vassals of the six houses. 

Alumni of the Society may return for events, visits, or even extended stays if they choose to.

## Life at the Society
Daily life at the Society is deliberately unstructured. There are no bells, mandatory assemblies, or attendance. Members are free to attend whatever they please and pursue their day however they like, be it dueling lessons, archery practice, research or sports. The only events of importance are the meals: seating may be informal, but watched. Who sits with whom matters. At night, the more daring members slip off the estate into Leonitar's less reputable establishments. Officially forbidden, this happens constantly.

At the conclusion of the eight year programme, the members attempt the Labyrinth of Chaos, a massive dungeon filled with monsters built beneath the Society. Surviving it allows a member to graduate; a gala is thrown at the end of every year to commemorate those who do. 

The gala is an elaborate dinner with the elders of the houses in attendance. It begins with a cohort procession, arranged not by house but by the bonds formed between the members over the eight years. Friends walk together. Rivals walk apart. The order is watched closely by the elders. It is followed by a toast from the Chairman of the Society, a rotating position held by a different house each year. The cohort then begins the first dance, an intense formal performance that is choreographed and rehearsed for weeks. As midnight approaches, the candles lit after the toast begin extinguishing themselves one by one, each representing a year of peace. The last candle, representing the current year, is left to burn through the night. The cohort then disperses into the gardens, into the night and their futures as heirs.
